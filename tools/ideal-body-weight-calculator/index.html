Formula Comparison

About the Formulas

Devine (1974)

Originally created to estimate drug dosing, it's the most widely used IBW formula in clinical settings.

Male: 50 kg + 2.3 × (inches over 5 ft)
Female: 45.5 kg + 2.3 × (inches over 5 ft)

Robinson (1983)

A refined version of Devine with slightly lower base values, often preferred in nutrition research.

Male: 52 kg + 1.9 × (inches over 5 ft)
Female: 49 kg + 1.7 × (inches over 5 ft)

Miller (1983)

Tends to give lower estimates; sometimes favored for smaller-framed populations.

Male: 56.2 kg + 1.41 × (inches over 5 ft)
Female: 53.1 kg + 1.36 × (inches over 5 ft)

Hamwi (1964)

One of the oldest formulas; adds a frame-size adjustment of ±10% for small/large frames.

Male: 48 kg + 2.7 × (inches over 5 ft)
Female: 45.4 kg + 2.3 × (inches over 5 ft)

⚠️ These formulas are reference tools, not medical advice. Ideal body composition varies by age, ethnicity, muscularity, and health status. Consult a healthcare professional for personalised guidance.

Frequently Asked Questions

Which formula is most accurate?

No single formula is universally accurate. Devine is the most cited in clinical pharmacology; Hamwi is common in nutrition counseling. Looking at the average of all four gives a practical target range.

How is ideal weight different from a healthy BMI weight?

BMI-based healthy weight (18.5–24.9) is sex-neutral and based solely on height. IBW formulas factor in biological sex and some account for frame size, which can make them more personalised.

Should I aim to reach my ideal body weight?

IBW is a guideline for clinical reference, not a strict goal. Achieving and maintaining a weight within a healthy BMI range (18.5–24.9) with good body composition is generally more important than hitting an exact IBW number.

Do these formulas work for children or teens?

These IBW formulas are validated for adults (18+). For children and adolescents, BMI-for-age percentile charts are the standard reference.
